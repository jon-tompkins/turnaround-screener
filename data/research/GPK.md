# GPK — Graphic Packaging Holding Company

**Research note · 2026-06-24 · high-leverage packaging turnaround with insider confirmation and cost-cut catalyst**

Price $10.53 · Mkt cap $3.11B · Total debt $5.79B · Net debt ~$5.60B · EV ~$8.63B
Revenue TTM $8.65B · Gross margin 17.0% · Operating margin 4.1% · FCF $187M · OCF $902M
EV/EBITDA ~7x · Forward P/E ~9x · P/B 0.96
Analyst consensus: none (9 analysts) · Mean target $11.79 (12% upside)

---

## Thesis in one paragraph

Graphic Packaging is one of the largest paperboard packaging companies in
North America, trading at 56% below its 52-week high and below book value
after a debt-fueled acquisition spree collided with margin compression from
input cost inflation. The company has $5.8B in debt against a $3.1B market
cap — the leverage is the story. But management has pivoted hard: capex cut
from $922M to $450M, $60M in cost reductions underway, a 90-day business
review completed, and directors are buying stock in cluster (30+ insider
purchase transactions in 6 months). If the $700-800M adjusted cash flow
guidance holds, the debt is manageable and the stock re-rates from 7x
EV/EBITDA toward 9-10x, offering 50-70% upside. If it doesn't, the debt
becomes a trap and equity holders get diluted. It's a binary setup.

## Why this is interesting

### 1. Insider buying is broad and sustained
- **348,464 shares purchased** across 32 transactions in 6 months; only
  46,898 shares sold (1 transaction)
- Notable director purchases:
  - **Jeffrey Stafeil** (Director): 16,261 shares at $9.84 ($160K) on May 20,
    plus 17,878 shares at $11.19 ($200K) on May 7
  - **Robert Hagemann** (Director): 14,000 shares at $11.00 ($154K) on May 8
  - **Larry Venturelli** (Director): 17,531 shares at $11.36 ($199K) on Mar 4
- Directors are putting personal capital into the stock at $9.84-$11.36 —
  the current price of $10.53 is **within their buy range**, meaning we're
  buying alongside them.

### 2. Capex cut is transformative for cash flow
- 2025 capex: $922M
- 2026 capex guidance: $450M — a **$472M reduction**
- Even with the Q1 net loss, operating cash flow was $902M TTM
- If capex drops to $450M and OCF holds, **free cash flow could surge from
  $187M to $450M+** — that changes the debt paydown trajectory materially
- Management reaffirmed $700-800M adjusted cash flow guidance for 2026

### 3. Cost reduction program is real and quantified
- $60M in staffing and other cost reductions expected in 2026
- $20M in severance and one-time/non-cash charges to implement
- Net $40M benefit to 2026 EBITDA — meaningful on a $350M EBITDA base
- 90-day comprehensive business review completed — suggests more initiatives
  could follow beyond the initial $60M

### 4. Trading below book value with real assets
- P/B = 0.96 — buying the equity for less than the accounting value of net
  assets
- The company has real manufacturing assets, a $8.65B revenue base, and an
  essential role in the food/beverage packaging supply chain
- This isn't a shell or a science experiment — it's a real business at a
  distressed multiple

### 5. Options are reasonably liquid
- 5 expiries available (through Jan 2027)
- 6,515 call OI, 893 put OI
- Enough liquidity for structured calls or leaps — better than most screener
  names for options strategies

## Why this could fail

### 1. The debt load is enormous and unforgiving
- $5.79B total debt vs $3.11B market cap — debt-to-equity of 178%
- A $400M delayed draw term loan matures June 2027 (refinancing the April
  2026 maturity) — this is the near-term refinancing risk
- If interest rates stay elevated, refinancing costs will remain a drag
- Interest expense eats most of the operating margin — at 4.1% operating
  margin on $8.65B revenue, that's ~$355M of operating income, much of which
  goes to interest

### 2. Q1 2026 was a net loss — turnaround not yet confirmed
- Q1 net loss of $43M despite 2% revenue growth
- EBITDA margin *fell* in Q1 — the cost cuts haven't shown up in results yet
- If Q2 doesn't show margin improvement from the $60M cost program, the
  market will lose patience

### 3. Commodity business with limited pricing power
- Paperboard packaging is price-competitive — customers (CPG companies) push
  back on price increases
- Input costs (pulp, energy, chemicals) are volatile and outside management's
  control
- If commodity inflation accelerates, margins compress further regardless of
  cost cuts

### 4. Short interest is high at 16% of float
- Bears are positioned for further downside — they see the debt overhang as
  an existential problem
- Any negative catalyst (guidance cut, refinancing difficulty) could trigger
  a short squeeze *downward* as momentum sellers pile in
- Short ratio of 5.12 days to cover — not extreme, but significant

### 5. Waco facility startup risk
- The Waco recycled paperboard facility is a major capital project
- Startup execution risk: delays, cost overruns, or quality issues could
  negate the capacity expansion thesis
- If the facility doesn't ramp as planned, the capex cut becomes harder to
  justify and growth investments look misallocated

## Position sizing

**Recommended: 2-3% of portfolio**

This is a high-risk, high-reward setup. The leverage means the equity could
go to near-zero in a worst case (debt trap), but the insider buying and cost
program suggest the downside is $7-8, not $0. Size it as a satellite position
— meaningful if it works, survivable if it doesn't.

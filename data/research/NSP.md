# NSP — Insperity, Inc.

**Research note · 2026-06-17 · PEO margin-recovery turnaround**

Price $38.02 · Mkt cap $1.45B · EV $1.21B · Cash $555M · Debt $430M
Float ~35.7M / 38.2M total shares · 6.2% insider held · ~112% institutional
Short interest 13.6% of float · CEO bought $12.6M in open-market purchases over 3 months

---

## The setup in one paragraph

Insperity is a PEO (professional employer organization) that provides full-service HR
outsourcing to small and midsize businesses. The stock has crashed 60%+ from $63 highs
after Q4 2025 showed a net loss driven by elevated benefits costs that crushed gross
margins. The turnaround thesis is straightforward: CEO Paul Sarvadi has bought $12.6M
of stock across two major purchases (March at ~$23 and June at $34), management has
taken concrete pricing and plan-design actions with UnitedHealthcare to recover
margins, and the new HRScale product (built on Workday) gives a growth lever beyond
cost recovery. The catalyst is Q2 2026 earnings on July 30 — the first clean quarter
with pricing actions fully in effect.

---

## Why this is interesting

### 1. CEO insider buying is massive and multi-round

Sarvadi bought $4.7M at a weighted average of ~$23 in March 2026, then another
$7.9M at $34.05 in June 2026 — his largest-ever open-market purchases. He previously
sold at $52 in September 2025. This isn't token insider buying; it's a founder-CEO
nearly doubling his position after the stock halved. The CFO, general counsel, and 8+
directors also bought in March. Cluster insider buying at multi-year lows with this
much conviction is unusual for a mid-cap PEO.

### 2. Margin recovery is mechanically achievable

The PEO model is simple: Insperity aggregates benefits purchasing for thousands of
SMB clients, then marks up the cost. When healthcare claims spiked in 2025 (inpatient,
outpatient, pharmacy trends, high-cost claims), gross margins compressed from ~17% to
~13%. Management's fix: (a) pricing increases on new and renewing clients, (b) plan
design changes with UnitedHealthcare to reduce claims exposure, (c) better client
selection (dropping unprofitable accounts). This isn't speculative — it's operational
mechanics. If gross margins recover from 13% toward 15-16%, operating leverage on $6.8B
in revenue is enormous.

### 3. HRScale is a real growth lever

HRScale, launched in 2025, combines Insperity's PEO expertise with Workday's
technology platform. This targets the mid-market (100-500 employees) that traditional
PEO hasn't served efficiently. Early traction isn't yet meaningful in reported
numbers, but if HRScale gains traction, it expands TAM significantly without the
same benefits cost exposure that crushed margins in 2024-25.

### 4. Forward P/E of ~13.6x is cheap for a recovering franchise

On a normalized earnings basis (which is the entire bet — can earnings normalize?),
NSP trades at roughly 13-14x forward P/E. For a company with $6.8B in revenue, a
structural industry tailwind (SMB outsourcing), and founder-CEO conviction evidenced
by $12.6M in purchases, that's a reasonable entry point — if the margin thesis plays
out.

### 5. PEO industry is structurally growing

The PEO industry grows at ~8-9% annually as SMBs increasingly outsource HR
compliance, benefits administration, and workforce management. Insperity is one of
the largest independent PEOs in the US. This isn't a declining business — it's a
growing business that had a cost shock.

---

## Why this could fail

### 1. Healthcare costs may stay elevated

The biggest risk is that benefits cost inflation is structural, not cyclical.
Inpatient utilization trends, specialty pharmacy costs, and high-cost claims
aren't obviously reverting. If Insperity's pricing actions don't keep pace,
margins stay compressed indefinitely.

### 2. Debt-to-equity of 641x is alarming

The balance sheet shows $430M in debt on thin equity (net losses eroded the equity
base). If recovery is delayed 2-3 quarters, covenant risk and balance sheet stress
become real concerns. Convertible debt or dilutive capital raises are possible.

### 3. HRScale rollout could be slow

Workday-powered mid-market products sound great, but PEO switching costs are high,
and mid-market clients have different economics than Insperity's traditional SME
base. If HRScale adoption is slower than expected, the growth narrative stall.

### 4. Thin analyst coverage = thin institutional sponsorship

Only 4 analysts cover NSP. The consensus target of $39.50 is barely above the
current price. This means limited institutional buying support even if the thesis
works — you're relying on price discovery rather than multiple expansion.

### 5. Razor-thin gross margins leave no room for error

At ~13% gross margin TTM, a 1-2 percentage point miss on benefits cost recovery
keeps the business barely profitable or loss-making. The PEO model's fundamental
vulnerability is that it's a pass-through business with thin markup.
